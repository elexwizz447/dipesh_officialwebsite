WHAT: Numerical Investigation and Parametric Study of Subsonic Tandem-Axial Flow Compressor for Aerodynamic Optimization

My undergraduate thesis addresses high-load compressor design challenges by developing a design methodology and performance model for a subsonic tandem-axial flow rotor to enhance stage pressure ratio while mitigating adverse pressure gradients, flow separation, and end-wall losses. Objectives include creating 2D/3D blade geometries using parabolic arc methods, conducting CFD simulations with ANSYS Fluent to analyze flow physics, and performing parametric studies on pitch percentage (PP) and axial overlap (AO) to optimize performance at on/off-design conditions.

HOW: Systematic Design and Numerical Methodology

Developed Python code for the design of 2D tandem airfoils and 3D rotors with spanwise variations in stagger, chord, reaction, and diffusion factor using velocity triangle model and parabolic arc methods, and incorporating AO/PP for boundary layer control.
Developed propulsion performance model and implemented CFD study in ANSYS Fluent, including grid independence studies, design space exploration, and passage flow analysis
Developed Python scripts for parametric analysis, varying PP (70-80%) and AO (-1 to 5%), evaluating metrics like pressure ratio, efficiency, and loss coefficient at on/off-design conditions for design optmization

RESULTS: Optimized Performance and Flow Insights

Performance Gains
Tandem rotor achieved higher pressure rise,better efficiency, and lower loss coefficient than conventional design, attributed to gap nozzle flow energizing boundary layers and reducing separation.
Parametric Analysis
Optimal 80% PP and 1% AO configuration increased pressure rise and efficiency, with wider stall-free range and minimized hub separation.
Flow Physics and Contributions
Analysis revealed performance improvement at engine level with a reduction in TSFC due to enhanced boundary layer control, reduced adverse pressure gradients, and mitigated end-wall losses, providing a preliminary design philosophy and performance model for tandem rotors and a Python GUI for flow angle assessment.

WHAT: Unified ANFIS-Based AVR‚ÄìLFC Control for Modern Power Systems

Developed an integrated MATLAB/Simulink framework to compare ANFIS and PID controllers for unified Automatic Voltage Regulation (AVR) and Load Frequency Control (LFC) in interconnected power systems. This work investigates how intelligent adaptive control responds to cross-coupled voltage‚Äìfrequency dynamics under load fluctuations.
The Matlab simulation environment models the generator swing dynamics, excitation and governor loops interconnection and per-unit (PU) load disturbances. Research outcomes involved nonlinear interactions between voltage and frequency control, the influence of inertia and damping on system stability, and the feasibility of ANFIS as a unified controller replacing traditional PID-only strategies.

WHAT: Understanding Grid-Forming Inverters in PV‚ÄìHydro Microgrids

This thesis explores how Grid-Forming (GFM) inverters and Virtual Synchronous Machine (VSM) control can support low inertia systems. For study Purpose a solar PV and low-inertia hydro generator has been considered. Many remote microgrids struggle with fast frequency changes and unstable voltage because the hydro unit does not have enough rotating mass to absorb disturbances. This work focuses on how a GFM inverter can act as the main voltage and frequency source, and how virtual inertia can make the system behave more like a traditional generator-based grid.

The study looks at the behavior of the combined PV‚ÄìHydro‚ÄìGFM system under realistic situations such as sudden load steps, cloud-driven PV changes, and weak-grid conditions. By studying these interactions, the thesis aims to give a clearer picture of how GFM‚ÄìVSM technology can make renewable-heavy microgrids more stable, more predictable, and more suitable for continuous operation in areas with limited electrical infrastructure.

Microgrid Overview

PV-Hydro Integration Model

HOW: Simulation & Modeling Approach (Ongoing Thesis Work)

Developing a dynamic model of the PV‚ÄìHydro microgrid, including a simplified swing equation to capture how virtual inertia interacts with the hydro generator‚Äôs real inertia.
Building a simulation environment where the GFM inverter forms voltage and frequency, while the hydro generator synchronizes to it and the PV inverter acts as a grid-following source.
Studying how the system reacts to disturbances such as PV fluctuations, load changes, and variations in hydro output, comparing GFM operation against traditional grid-following control.
Since this work is part of an ongoing thesis, the models, tuning methods, and simulation cases are being expanded and refined step-by-step to reach more realistic conclusions and system understanding.

RESULTS: Key Findings & Early Technical Insights

Improved Frequency Behavior
Early simulations show that the GFM‚ÄìVSM inverter provides fast support during sudden load or PV changes, reducing frequency dips and keeping the microgrid more stable than the hydro unit alone.
Better Coordination With the Hydro Generator
When the inverter forms the grid, the hydro generator synchronizes more smoothly, experiences fewer oscillations, and avoids the ‚Äúhunting‚Äù behavior seen in low-inertia setups.
Ride-Through Performance
The PV inverter follows IEEE 2800 ride-through standards, remaining connected during voltage and frequency disturbances within limits.Initial simulations show that the controller continuously supplies the reference current. While the full validation is still in progress, the thesis findings so far support the idea that grid-forming control is a practical and promising solution for small renewable microgrids.
